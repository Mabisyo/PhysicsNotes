# Electric Charge
## Definitions and Concepts
- The Electric Charge gives protons and electrons an attractive force. Particles of the same charge will always repel each other, and like-charge particles will always attract each other.
- ==Protons: Positive (+)==
- ==Electrons: Negative (-)==
- In order for matter to be **charged** there must be an imbalance between the number of protons and electrons, caused by removal or addition of electrons.
- **Conservation of Charge**: Net charge in an isolated system remains constant
- The symbol and value of the elementary charge is:
$$ e = 1.602(10)^{-19} \space [C]$$
- **Quantization of Charge**: Charge exists in discrete packets of electron charge, because charge can only be added or subtracted in units of e.
- The symbol to denote the charge of a particle is q.
- **Conductors vs. Insulators**: Conductors allow free movement of electrons (e.g. metals). Insulators resist electron flow (e.g. rubber, glass)
# Coulomb’s Law (Electric Force between Point Charges)
## Definitions and Concepts
- The electric force between two charged particles obeys a law similar to Newton’s Law of Gravitation: inverse-square laws.
- Coulomb’s Law measures the electric force between two particles with charges of q1 and q2 separated by some distance, r.
- When using Coulomb’s Law we interpret a negative output as an attraction between the charges, and a positive output as a repulsion.
## Formulas and Symbols
- Coulomb’s Law:$$\vec F_e = k_e \frac{q_1q_2}{r^2}\hat r = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{q_1q_2}{r^2} \hat r$$
	- k is the proportionality constant known as Coulomb’s Constant. However, it will be expressed in terms of a fundamental constant known as the permittivity of free space. $$k = 9(10)^9 \space [\frac{N*m^2}{C^2}] = \frac{1}{4\pi \epsilon_0}$$ 
- k sub e is Coulomb’s Constant
- The “q” represent charges (C)
- r represents the distance between charges (m)
- r with wide-hat represents the unit vector in the direction of the force


## Electric Field (E) due to Point Charges
**Definition**: Force per unit charge experienced by a test charge (initial charge)
**Equation**: 
$$
	\vec{E}=\frac{\vec{F}_e}{q_0}=k_e\frac{q}{r^2}\widehat{r}
$$
***Superposition of Fields***
$$
	\vec{E}\tiny net \normalsize = \sum{\vec{E}_i}
$$
## Key Derivations
**1. Deriving Coulomb’s Law from Gauss’s Law**
- Gauss’s Law: $$\intop{\vec{E}(d\vec{A})=\frac{Q\tiny enc \normalsize}{\epsilon_0}} $$
- For a point charge, symmetry gives: 
$$
	E(4\pi r^2) = \frac{q}{\epsilon_0} \rightarrowtail E = (\frac{1}{4\pi \epsilon_0})(\frac{q}{r^2})=k_e\frac{q}{r^2}
$$
- Thus, force on another charge q initial is: 
$$
	F_e = q_0E=k_e\frac{qq_0}{r^2}
$$
**2. Electric Field of a Dipole**
*For two equal and opposite charges plus or minus q separated by distance d:*
- Along the axial line (x-axis):
$$
	E\tiny dipole \normalsize = \frac{2k_ep}{x^3}; x>>d
$$
where p=qd (dipole moment)
- Along the perpendicular bisector (y-axis)
$$
	E \tiny dipole \normalsize = \frac{k_ep}{y^3}
$$

# Problem-Solving Strategies
______________
**A. Quick Calculations**
- Symmetry Use: If charges are symmetric, cancel out components
	- Geometric properties of a charge distribution that are preserved when a tranformation is applied.
- Unit Vectors: Break forces into x,y,z components
- Approximations: For r >> d, use binomial expansion
**B. Common Pitfalls**
- Sign Errors: Remember force direction (attraction vs. repulsion)
- Units: Always use SI 
- Superposition: Don’t forget vector addition
