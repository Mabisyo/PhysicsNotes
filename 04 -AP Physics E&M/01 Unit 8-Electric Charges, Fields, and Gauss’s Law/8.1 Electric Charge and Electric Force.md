# Electric Charge
_______________________________
## Definitions and Concepts
* A fundamental property of matter that causes it to experience electromagnetic forces. It is not the same as Energy, Force, Motion, or Magnetism, it simply describes how particles interact electrically.
* Charges include: Positive (Protons), Neutral (Neutrons), and Negative (Electrons)
* Protons and Electrons have charges with opposite signs and equal magnitudes.
* **Conservation of Charge**: Net charge in an isolated system remains constant.
* **Quantization of Charge**: Charge exists in packets of charge, the fundamental unit of charge is called the elementary charge, defined by (e):
$$e \space = \space 1.602 \times 10^{-19} \space C_{oulombs}$$
# Coulomb’s Law
* This equation defines the electric force between 2 particles of q charge at r distance. Electric force is proportional to electric force, decreases with distance squared (inverse square law), and acts along line r. In order to determine where the force points, recall that opposite charges point towards each other, while same charges repel each other. This equation calculates the magnitude of the force, and the r hat is defined by you determining if the force points in between charges, or away from each other.

$$
\vec{F}_e = k_e\frac{|q_1q_2|}{r^2}\widehat{r}
$$
- k sub e is Coulomb’s Constant (provided on Equation sheet)
- The “q” represent charge (C) of 1 particle
- r represents the distance between charges (m)
- r with wide-hat represents the unit vector in the direction of the force

# Superposition Principle
The idea that multiple charges do not interfere with each other, they simply add together. Since Electric forces are linear, they add together. Each force is calculated as if the others did not exist, then you add vectors. This is because electric forces obey linear differential equations (more on that in Gauss’s Law). So if they pull opposite, they cancel, if they pull at angles, use components. This applies to Forces, Electric Fields, Electric Potential, and Magnetic Fields.
