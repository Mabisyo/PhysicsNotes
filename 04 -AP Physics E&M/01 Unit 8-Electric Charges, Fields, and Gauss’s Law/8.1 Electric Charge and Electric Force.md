# Electric Charge
**What is Electric Charge?**
**Definition**: A fundamental property of matter that causes it to experience electromagnetic forces. It is not the same as Energy, Force, Motion, or Magnetism, it simply describes how particles interact electrically.
**Types of Charges**
- Positive (+): Protons
- Negative (-): Electrons
- Neutral: Neutrons
The Proton and Electron have equal magnitude and opposite sign charge.
**Conservation of Charge**: Net charge in an isolated system remains constant
**Quantization of Charge**: Charge exists in discrete packets (8.2) of electron charge, called an elementary charge…
$$ e = 1.602(10)^{-19}C$$
The unit is in Coulombs, which is 1 Amp * 1 Second.

**What does Electric Charge Do?**
Electric charges create electric force, which can either repel or attract. Like charges repel, and opposite charges attract.

# Superposition Principle
The idea that multiple charges do not interfere with each other, they simply add together. Since Electric forces are linear, they add together. Each force is calculated as if the others did not exist, then you add vectors. This is because electric forces obey linear differential equations (more on that in Gauss’s Law). So if they pull opposite, they cancel, if they pull at angles, use components. This applies to Forces, Electric Fields, Electric Potential, and Magnetic Fields.

# Key Derivations
**1. Deriving Coulomb’s Law from Gauss’s Law (Not Mandatory for AP Exam)**
- Gauss’s Law: $$\intop{\vec{E}(d\vec{A})=\frac{Q\tiny enc \normalsize}{\epsilon_0}} $$
- For a point charge, symmetry gives: 
$$
	E(4\pi r^2) = \frac{q}{\epsilon_0} \rightarrowtail E = (\frac{1}{4\pi \epsilon_0})(\frac{q}{r^2})=k_e\frac{q}{r^2}
$$
- Thus, force on another charge q initial is: 
$$
	F_e = q_0E=k_e\frac{qq_0}{r^2}
$$
**2. Electric Field of a Dipole**
*For two equal and opposite charges plus or minus q separated by distance d:*
- Along the axial line (x-axis):
$$
	E\tiny dipole \normalsize = \frac{2k_ep}{x^3}; x>>d
$$
where p=qd (dipole moment)
- Along the perpendicular bisector (y-axis)
$$
	E \tiny dipole \normalsize = \frac{k_ep}{y^3}
$$
3. **Acceleration of Any Two Charged Spheres**
- Start with Coulomb’s Law & Equation for Gravity: $$\sum{F_e}=-F_g-F_e$$
$$m_e\vec{a}=-\frac{GM_eM_p}{r^2}-(\frac{kQQ}{r^2})$$
- Multiply both sides by mass: $$\vec{a}=\frac{GM_p}{r^2}-\frac{kQQ}{M_er^2}$$



**Conductors vs. Insulators**: 
- Conductors allow free movement of electrons (e.g. metals)
- Insulators resist electron flow (e.g. rubber, glass)

# Problem-Solving Strategies
______________
**A. Quick Calculations**
- Symmetry Use: If charges are symmetric, cancel out components
	- Geometric properties of a charge distribution that are preserved when a tranformation is applied.
- Unit Vectors: Break forces into x,y,z components
- Approximations: For r >> d, use binomial expansion
**B. Common Pitfalls**
- Sign Errors: Remember force direction (attraction vs. repulsion)
- Units: Always use SI 
- Superposition: Don’t forget vector addition
