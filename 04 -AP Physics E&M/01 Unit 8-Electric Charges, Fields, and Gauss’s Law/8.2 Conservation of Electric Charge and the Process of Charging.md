# Conservation of Change
- Charge is always conserved and constant within an isolated system. This a fundamental law of nature, charge cannot be created nor destroyed, just transferred. 
  
  This means that if something has a negative net charge, that means it gained electrons, not gained negative energy. When something has a positive charge, it means it lost electrons. PROTONS DO NOT MOVE, charge transfer = electron transfer.
  
  ## Ways Charge is Lost
- Charge is still conserved, but can be exchanged with its surroundings, such as the environment not included in the system.
- Grounding: Connecting a charged object to a much larger and “neutral” object, allowing charge to flow out and charges to neutralize in the object. Traditionally, this is when a wire is connected to the ground, hence the term, “grounding.”
  
# Quantization of Charge
Charge is not a continuous amount at a microscopic scale. Since you cannot cut an electron in half and get half a charge, we can conceptually understand charge as little packets, each packet being 1 electron. The equation would be as follows.
$$q=ne$$
Where q is the charge, n is an integer, and e is the elementary charge as discussed in 8.1.


# Conductors Vs Insulators
Conductors are materials that allow charges to move freely through them, due to the fact they have “delocalized electrons” that are free and not bound anywhere, allowing them to move freely. When a conductor is charged, the charge spreads evenly across its surface, however inside the conductor, the electric field is zero, when at electrostatic equilibrium. They can be charged by conduction and induction.

Insulators are materials that do not allow charges to move freely through them, since electrons are tightly bound to atoms. Charge does not move freely and stays localized, they cannot easily transfer charge, but they can still be charged by friction.

- Polarization: When charges shift inside a neutral object so that one side becomes more positive and the other more negative, without electrons leaving the object. Usually requires an object with a charge outside of the object being polarized.
# Methods of Charging

- ***Friction***: Brushing electrons onto another object. One becomes positive, the other becomes negative, but total charge remains zero in the system.

- ***Conduction***: Charged objects touches a conductor with a neutral charge, transferring charge to reach equilibrium (the same charge between the 2 objects). Requires direct contact.

- ***Induction***: Occurs when an object with a charge is brought near a conductor with a neutral charge, does not require direct contact. If the charged object has a negative charge, then electrons inside the conductor will move to the side that is further away from the negative charge, causing a negative charge on the side furthest from the charged object, and a positive charge closest to the charged object. If you were to then connect the conductor with a wire to the ground, the electrons will leave the conductor. Removing the wire from the ground and moving away the charged object will result in an overall positive net charge.


- Understand that Conductors and Insulators can both be polarized, but only a conductor can be charged using induction.

## Why do Electrons Move?
- Electrons move because electric forces act upon them. Charges push or pull other charges, which is defined by the magnitude and direction of the electric force.
- When a conductor or object has extra charge, that charge creates an electric field around it, which points away from positive charges and toward negative charges. 
- Since electrons in a conductor are free to move, in the presence of an electric field, these electrons move towards positive regions and away from negative ones.
- They do this until there is no movement (all electrons get to where they want to be), and the charges reach electrostatic equilibrium.
