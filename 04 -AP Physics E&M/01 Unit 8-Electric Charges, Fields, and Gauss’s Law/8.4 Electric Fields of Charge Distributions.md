# Charge Density
- When given a charged object, the charge isn’t always uniformly distributed throughout the object. Therefore, charge density represents how a charge is distributed through space and/or an object. 
- On the AP exam, they might give you an equation for charge density, in which you need to work with in order to determine the eletric field.
- The units are in coulombs per length/area/volume, (C/m^n, where n=1,2,3). The 3 types discussed in this class are…  
## Linear Charge Density
- Charge density in 1 dimension, can be dx, dy, dr, or dL (rarely), depending on context. Units are C/m
$$\lambda=\frac{Q}{L}=\frac{dq}{dr}$$
## Area Charge Density
- Charge density in 2 dimensions. Units are C/m^2
$$\sigma=\frac{Q}{A}=\frac{dq}{dA}$$
## Volume Charge Density
- Charge density in 3 dimensions. Units are C/m^3
$$\rho=\frac{Q}{V}=\frac{dq}{dV}$$
Understand conceptually, that this equation simply states that for every infinitesimally small change in charge, there is an infinitesimally small change in unit of measurement. By algebraic manipulation, we can find a substitute for dq to use in the integral form of the electric field equation.

# Integral Form
- For unit 8.3, we were working with the electric field created by a “point charge,” or an individual particle’s charge. However, in real world applications, objects that have electric fields are not one individual point charge, but a sum of numerous charges. When calculating the electric field, we need to work with the integral form of the electric field equation.$$\vec{E}=\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\intop{\frac{dQ}{r^2}}\hat{r}$$
- However as mentioned before, we need to work with charge density, symmetry, and geometric understandings of what we are integrating. Understanding the derivations below is essential for the Ap exam. Know all 3.
# Superposition in Integral Form
$$\vec{E}_{net}=\intop{(\vec{E_i})\hat i}$$
Recall that superposition is just the sum of all electric forces and or fields. We can represent this concept with an integral. The i hat represents the direction, which is defined by your coordinate axis.
# E-Field at a point Colinear with a line of Charge
![[efieldatapointcolinearwithalineofcharge.png]]
The following is a question directly from the College Board Ap classroom. Follow the derivation steps. 

1. First, recall the formula for linear charge density, shown below. $$\lambda=\frac{Q}{L}=\frac{dq}{dr}$$
   Through simple rearrangement, we can find a substitute for dq,
   $$dq=\frac{Qdr}{L}$$
   Notice how we did not use dL. L is a finite measurement, based on the length of the rod. Using dx would also be incorrect, since x is also a finite value in this question. Using dr would be the best option, since we are working along the r vector.

2. Next, recall the E-Field integral, and substitute dq.
   $$\vec{E}=\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\intop{\frac{dQ}{r^2}}\hat{r}$$  $$\vec{E}=\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\intop{\frac{Qdr}{Lr^2}}\hat{r}$$
3. Move the constants out of the integral, and define the integral bounds.$$\vec{E}=\frac{Q}{4\pi\epsilon_0L}\intop_{x}^{L+x}{\frac{dr}{r^2}}\hat{r}$$
   Understanding where the integral bounds come from depends on your coordinate axis. Imagine that the origin of the coordinate axis is at the observation point to the right of the rod, and the left is the positive direction. There is no charge outside of the object, so we need to start at where the object starts at, so at distance x away from the origin, meaning the lower bound should be x. Now, we are integrating along the entire rod, which has a distance L, so the upper bound should be x+L. 


4. We now need to integrate and plug in the necessary bounds, which will yield our final answer. $$E=\frac{Q}{4\pi\epsilon_oL}\left(\frac{1}{x}-\frac{1}{L+x}\right)\hat{r}$$
   Which can be algebraically simplified to…
$$E=\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\left(\frac{Q}{x(L+x)}\right)\hat{r}$$
This is a general equation that applies to all colinear point charges, though it is recommended to understand how to get to this point, rather than memorize the equation.

5. Understanding edge conditions (limits as certain variables approach different values) allows us to grasp a better conceptual understanding.
   
   If L<<<X, then we can say L approaches zero, which leaves us with the same equation for that of a point charge. $$\vec{E}=\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\left({\frac{Q}{x^2}}\right)\hat{r}$$
   If L>>>>X, then our denominator will go to infinity, meaning our E-field would approach zero.
   
   
# Charges of rings and partial rings
   Say there is a circular ring with Radius R, and Linear charge density Lambda. If we were to try and find the eletric field at the center of the ring, what would it be?
   - The answer would be zero. Due to the symmetry of the ring, the eletric field created by the top of the ring would cancel with the bottom of the ring, and the same with the horizontal components of the circle.

However, say we have a ring with part of the ring missing. How would we calculate the electric field? See the example below. The example is taken directly from the College Board’s AP Classroom.
![[partialringofchargewith60degreesmissing.png]]
A Partial ring has a radius R, and a linear charge density of +Lambda. The ring is missing a 60 degree arc on the right side as shown. What is the electric field on the arc as shown?

1. To start, we need to recognize that our charge density is linear with respect to arc length, not with respect to area. The equation would be shown below…$$\lambda=\frac{dq}{ds}\rightarrow\lambda ds = dq$$Where ds is an infinitesimally small amount of arc length.

2. However, we are not given the section of arc length missing in the problem, instead we are given the angle, so we need to introduce a new substitution.$$r=\frac{s}{\theta}$$
   Since we know that R is constant, we can turn s and theta into differentials and isolate ds, where we can plug that into ds.$$Rd\theta=ds\rightarrow \lambda Rd\theta=dq$$
3. Next, recall the superposition formula for eletric fields. $$\vec{E}_{net}=\intop{(\vec{E_i}\hat)}$$
   In this step, we need to recognize the symmetry of the ring. Since there is a line of symmetry that is directly horizontal through the ring, then that means the electric field created by the top half of the ring is canceled by the bottom half of the ring. However, there is vertical line of symmetry, meaning that the horizontal components of the ring do not cancel, meaning that we need to take into account the horizontal components of the Electric field. Recall components. $$E\cos(\theta)=E_x$$
   After plugging in for E_x, we are left with a new formula. $$\vec{E}_{net}=\vec{E_x}=\intop{\vec{E_x}}=\intop{(E\cos{\theta})\hat i}$$
4. Recalling the integral for Electric field, we can substitute this in as well.
$$\vec{E}=\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\intop{\frac{dQ}{r^2}}\hat{r}$$ $$\vec{E}_{net}=\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\intop\frac{dQ\cos{\theta}}{r^2}{\hat r}$$
Please note that r hat and I hat are interchangeable, and you may even see different variables representing different axes. The general formula uses i hat for the superposition formula, while this formula uses r hat. They can be used interchangeably, as long as you mention the direction at the end of the problem. 

5. Now, we can substitute in dQ that we solved for earlier, and move constants outside of the integral.
 $$\vec{E}_{net}=\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\intop\frac{\lambda Rd\theta\cos{\theta}}{R^2}{\hat r}$$
Also note that r=R, as defined in the question. This is not always the case, some questions have multiple radius, but there is only one radius in this question, that being the radius of the circle. This means that one of the Rs in the denominator will cancel with one in the numerator. After moving constants out of the integral, we are left with.
 $$\vec{E}_{net}=\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{\lambda}{R}\intop\cos{\theta}d \theta$$
 6. Next, we need to define our bounds using geometry. Recall that only the right side of the circle is missing. We know that we are missing 60 degrees, but if our bounds were 0 to 60, that would incorrect, since you would integrate from the ride side of the right up to the upper right corner (0 to Pi/3 on the unit circle, which is incorrect). We need to integrate the part of the ring that is missing, which would be starting at -30 degrees, and going to 30. We can setup this integral 2 ways, with 2 different bounds. They will both yield the same answer. $$\vec{E}_{net}=\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{\lambda}{R}\intop_{-30}^{30}\cos{\theta}d \theta$$$$\vec{E}_{net}=\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{\lambda}{R}2\intop_{0}^{30}\cos{\theta}d \theta$$
 7. After integrating and simplifying, your final answer gives…$$\vec{E}=\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{\lambda}{R}\hat i$$
 8. Say 300 degrees of the ring are missing, leaving only the left side of the circle present. Our bounds would change to -150 to 150, which when evaluating gives you the same answer as
 $$\vec{E}=\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{\lambda}{R}\hat i$$
 This means that if 1/6th of the ring is missing, it would generate the same e field as a ring with 5/6ths of the ring missing. So how would we maximize the e-field?
 
 - Removing 1/2 of the ring will leave you with the greatest e-field… $$\vec{E}=\frac{1}{2\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{\lambda}{R}\hat i$$
# Point along the axis of a ring
![[pointalongtheaxisofaringelectricfield.png]]
Say you are given the following example, in which we need to find the eletric field at point P. The following example is directly from the College Board’s AP Classroom.

1.  First, we need to work on our substitution. We are again working with linear charge density, but instead, we are working the circumference of a ring with a radius R.
   $$\lambda=\frac{-Q}{2\pi R}=\frac{dQ}{ds}$$ $$dQ=\frac{-Qds}{2\pi R}$$
2. We now need to define new measurements, such as a diagonal radius “r” that goes from point P to touching the ring. ![[pointalongtheaxisofaringannotated.png]]
   We can now define radius “r” in terms of x and R, using the Pythagorean theorem. $$r=\left(x^2+R^2\right)^{\frac{1}{2}}$$
   The reason we did this is because in nature, fundamental forces have the tendency to act through the most direct path. Due to the electric field taking the most direct path to point P, we need to find the shortest distance away the ring is from point P, which would be the hypotenuse of the triangle formed with legs X and R.

3. Similar to the rings we worked with before, notice how we can break the eletric field into vertical and horizontal components. After doing so, since the ring has no holes in it, the vertical components of the electric field will cancel itself out, leaving only the X-Components. We can use the derivation from the earlier question to get…$$\vec{E}_{net}=\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\intop\frac{dQ\cos{\theta}}{r^2}{\hat r}$$
   However, we are not given the cosine formed by the angle, just distances X and R. However, we can use trigonometry to help us out.
   $$\cos(\theta)=\frac{adj}{hyp}=\frac{x}{r}$$
   Since we are working with the angle at point P, we would be able to use trigonometry to substitute x and r for cosine.

4. Now that we have all necessary substitutions, we can create our formula, and move the constants outside of our integral. 
   $$\vec{E}_{net}=\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\intop\frac{x}{(x^2+R^2)^{\frac{1}{2}}}\frac{\frac{-Qds}{2\pi R}}{(x^2+r^2)}\hat i$$
   $$\vec{E}_{net}=\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\int\frac{-Qx}{2\pi R(x^2+R^2)^{\frac{3}{2}}}ds \space \hat i$$  
   $$\vec{E}_{net}=-\frac{xQ}{8\pi^2\epsilon_0R(x^2+R^2)^{\frac{3}{2}}}\hat i\intop ds$$
4. Now, we need to identify the bounds of the integral. Because we are integrating the entire arc length of the ring, our lower bound must be zero, and our upper bound should be the entire ring, which is the circumference, or 2piR.
   $$\vec{E}_{net}=-\frac{xQ}{8\pi^2\epsilon_0R(x^2+R^2)^{\frac{3}{2}}}\hat i\intop_{0}^{2\pi R} ds$$
5. After evaluating the integral and simplifying, we get…
$$\vec{E}_{net}=-\frac{xQ}{4\pi\epsilon_0(x^2+R^2)^{\frac{3}{2}}}\hat i$$
Another general equation that works in these types of situations. This is the hardest derivation so far, so understanding how to get here is important.
