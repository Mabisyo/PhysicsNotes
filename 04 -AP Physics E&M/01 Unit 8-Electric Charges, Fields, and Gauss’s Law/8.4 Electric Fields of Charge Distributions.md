# Charge Density
- When given a charged object, the charge isn’t always uniformly distributed throughout the object. Therefore, charge density represents how a charge is distributed through space and/or an object. 
- On the AP exam, they might give you an equation for charge density, in which you need to work with in order to determine the eletric field.
- The units are in coulombs per length/area/volume, (C/m^n, where n=1,2,3). The 3 types discussed in this class are…  
## Linear Charge Density
- Charge density in 1 dimension, can be dx, dy, dr, or dL (rarely), depending on context. Units are C/m
$$\lambda=\frac{Q}{L}=\frac{dq}{dr}$$
## Area Charge Density
- Charge density in 2 dimensions. Units are C/m^2
$$\sigma=\frac{Q}{A}=\frac{dq}{dA}$$
## Volume Charge Density
- Charge density in 3 dimensions. Units are C/m^3
$$\rho=\frac{Q}{V}=\frac{dq}{dV}$$
Understand conceptually, that this equation simply states that for every infinitesimally small change in charge, there is an infinitesimally small change in unit of measurement. By algebraic manipulation, we can find a substitute for dq to use in the integral form of the electric field equation.

# Integral Form
- For unit 8.3, we were working with the electric field created by a “point charge,” or an individual particle’s charge. However, in real world applications, objects that have electric fields are not one individual point charge, but a sum of numerous charges. When calculating the electric field, we need to work with the integral form of the electric field equation.$$\vec{E}=\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\intop{\frac{dQ}{r^2}}\hat{r}$$
- However as mentioned before, we need to work with charge density, symmetry, and geometric understandings of what we are integrating. Understanding the derivations below is essential for the Ap exam. Know all 3.

# E-Field at a point Colinear with a line of Charge
![[efieldatapointcolinearwithalineofcharge.png]]
The following is a question directly from the College Board Ap classroom. Follow the derivation steps. 

1. First, recall the formula for linear charge density, shown below. $$\lambda=\frac{Q}{L}=\frac{dq}{dr}$$
   Through simple rearrangement, we can find a substitute for dq,
   $$dq=\frac{Qdr}{L}$$
   Notice how we did not use dL. L is a finite measurement, based on the length of the rod. Using dx would also be incorrect, since x is also a finite value in this question. Using dr would be the best option, since we are working along the r vector.

2. Next, recall the E-Field integral, and substitute dq.
   $$\vec{E}=\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\intop{\frac{dQ}{r^2}}\hat{r}$$  $$\vec{E}=\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\intop{\frac{Qdr}{Lr^2}}\hat{r}$$
3. Move the constants out of the integral, and define the integral bounds.$$\vec{E}=\frac{Q}{4\pi\epsilon_0L}\intop_{x}^{L+x}{\frac{dr}{r^2}}\hat{r}$$
   Understanding where the integral bounds come from depends on your coordinate axis. Imagine that the origin of the coordinate axis is at the observation point to the right of the rod, and the left is the positive direction. There is no charge outside of the object, so we need to start at where the object starts at, so at distance x away from the origin, meaning the lower bound should be x. Now, we are integrating along the entire rod, which has a distance L, so the upper bound should be x+L. 


4. We now need to integrate and plug in the necessary bounds, which will yield our final answer. $$E=\frac{Q}{4\pi\epsilon_oL}\left(\frac{1}{x}-\frac{1}{L+x}\right)\hat{r}$$
   Which can be algebraically simplified to…
$$E=\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\left(\frac{Q}{x(L+x)}\right)\hat{r}$$
This is a general equation that applies to all colinear point charges, though it is recommended to understand how to get to this point, rather than memorize the equation.

5. Understanding edge conditions (limits as certain variables approach different values) allows us to grasp a better conceptual understanding.
   
   If L<<<X, then we can say L approaches zero, which leaves us with the same equation for that of a point charge. $$\vec{E}=\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\left({\frac{Q}{x^2}}\right)\hat{r}$$
   If L>>>>X, then our denominator will go to infinity, meaning our E-field would approach zero.
