# Concepts
_________________
*When current flows through a coil, it creates a magnetic field through itself. If that current changes the flux through the coil changes, and by Faraday’s Law an EMF is induced in the coil itself - this is **Self Inductance.** The induced EMF always opposes the change in current (Lenz’s Law), making the inductor resist both increases and decreases in current.*
## Inductors 
*An inductor is a circuit element that opposes changes in current* by generating a back EMF(∈). Energy stored in an inductor lives in its magnetic field, exactly as energy stored in a capacitor lives in its electric field. 
#### Solenoid
*Solenoids consist of a wire tightly wrapped around a cylindrical core to create a helical stack of conducting loop. When current is established in the solenoid, a magnetic field exists through the core.* $$L = \frac{N\phi}{I}$$
- **Magnetic Field Strength inside a Solenoid**: $$B_{sol}=\mu_{0}\frac{N}{l}I$$
#### Solenoid as an Inductor
- A dielectric could increase the capacitance of a capacitor $$C = \frac{\kappa \epsilon_{0}A}{d}$$
- A different material, other than air or a vacuum, in the core of a solenoid can increase its inductance. $$L_{sol}=\frac{\mu_{core}N^2A}{l}$$
If current changes, the flux through the coil changes, and by Faraday’s Law an EMF is induced in the coil itself. This is called self inductance. The induced EMF always opposes the change in current (Lenz’s Law), making the inductor “sluggish,” it resists both increases and decreases in current. 
- When a current exists inside the coils of a solenoid, a magnetic field B exists through the core of the solenoid. 
- Energy is stored in this magnetic field. 
#### Induced emf in a Circuit
- Inductors are used in circuits
- As current changes, the inductor self-induces an emf that will oppose any change in current. 
- If current increases, an emf is induced across the inductor. 
- If current decreases, an emf is induced across the inductor.
#### Self Inductance
$$ \epsilon_{L} = -L\frac{dI}{dt}$$
- With a link to flux: $$L = \frac{N \phi_{B}}{I}$$
	- N = number of turns
	- Inductance depends only on geometry - not on current or time

#### Inductance of an Ideal Solenoid - Derivation
*Given: Solenoid length l, cross-sectional area A, N total turns (n/l turns per meter), carrying current I*
1. B-field inside solenoid from Ampere’s Law: $$B = \mu_{0}nI=\mu_{0}\frac{N}{l}I$$
2. Flux per turn $$\phi_{B}=BA=\mu_{0} \frac{N}{l}IA$$
3. Total Flux Linkage $$N \phi_{B}=\mu_{0} \frac{N^2A}{l}I$$
4. Inductance (Result) $$L = \frac{N\phi_{B}}{I}=\frac{\mu_{0}N^2A}{l}= \mu_{0}n^2Al$$
	1. L scales with N squared - doubling turns quadruples inductance L. L depends only on geometry (N, A, *l*) and the permeability of free space.
#### Energy Stored in an Inductor
*Work must be done against the back EMF to build up current from 0 to I*: $$\begin{array}
dW = \epsilon_{L}Idt = L \frac{dI}{dt}Idt = LIdI \\
U_{B}= \int_{0}^{I} = LI'dI' = \frac{1}{2}LI^2 \\
U_{B}= \frac{1}{2}LI^2
\end{array}$$
- Energy is sore in the magnetic field of the inductor
- When current stops, this energy must go somewhere (dissipated as heat, spark, or transferred to capacitor)
- “Inductive kick”: sudden disconnection of an inductor causes a huge voltage spike as it tries to maintain current. 
#### Energy Storage Devices – The Capacitor
- When a capacitor is charged it possesses some potential difference delta V and an electric field exists between the places.
- Energy is stored in this electric field. 
$$U_{C}= \frac{1}{2}Q\Delta V$$

#### Magnetic Energy Density
Energy is distributed throughout the volume of the magnetic field: $$u_{B}= \frac{U_{B}}{V_{solenoid}}=\frac{\frac{1}{2}LI^2}{Al}$$- Substitute 
$$\begin{array}
L = \mu_{0}n^2Al \space {and} \space B = \mu_{0nI} \\
u_{B} = \frac{B^2}{2\mu_{0}}
\end{array}$$
- Valid anywhere there is a magnetic field - not just in solenoids
- Analogous to electric energy density
#### Inductor Behavior in DC Circuits
**At t = 0 (Immediately after switch closes)**: 
- Current through inductor is zero (cannot change instantaneously)
- Inductor acts like an open circuit – the back EMF equals the supply voltage
- All voltage drops across the inductor: $$V_{L} = \epsilon$$
**At t → ∞ (Steady State, DC)**
- Current is constant and back EMF is zero
- Inductor acts like a short circuit (just a wire)
- All voltage drops across resistor: $$V_{R}=\epsilon$$
***NOTE: Current through an inductor is always continuous. It cannot jump instantaneously. This is the inductor’s defining constraint, equivalent to the capacitor rule that voltage cannot jump.***
#### Inductance Geometry Summary

| Geometry                                         | Inductance Formula                                  | Notes                        |
| ------------------------------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------- |
| Ideal solenoid                                   | $L = \mu_0 n^2 A \ell = \frac{\mu_0 N^2 A}{\ell}$   | Most common on AP exam       |
| Toroid (N turns, inner radius a, outer radius b) | $L = \frac{\mu_0 N^2 h}{2\pi}\ln\frac{b}{a}$        | Less common — know it exists |
| Single circular loop                             | $L \approx \mu_0 r\left(\ln\frac{8r}{a} - 2\right)$ | Rarely needed                |

## Key Vocabulary

|Term|Definition|
|---|---|
|**Self-Inductance (L)**|Property of a coil to oppose changes in its own current; $L = N\Phi_B / I$|
|**Henry (H)**|SI unit of inductance; 1 H = 1 V·s/A = 1 Wb/A|
|**Back-EMF**|Self-induced EMF opposing the change in current; $\varepsilon_L = -L,dI/dt$|
|**Flux Linkage (NΦ)**|Total flux threading all N turns of a coil|
|**Energy Density (u_B)**|Magnetic energy per unit volume; $u_B = B^2/2\mu_0$|
|**Mutual Inductance (M)**|Coupling between two coils; changing current in one induces EMF in the other|
|**Inductive Kick**|Large voltage spike when current through an inductor is suddenly interrupted|
