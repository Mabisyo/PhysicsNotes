# Concepts
_________________
Note: When a conductor moves through a magnetic field, free charges inside it experience a magnetic force – this separates charge, creates an EMF, and if the circuit is closed, drives a real current. That current then feels its own magnetic force, which always opposes the original motion (Lenz’s Law). Mechanical energy converts to electrical energy, and then to heat. 
- Anytime magnetic flux changes through a conducting loop, the induced emf results in an induced current
#### EMF
$$emf=\epsilon=N|\frac{d\phi}{dt}|$$
- As a loop exits a field, magnetic flux changes, resulting in an induced emf. 
#### Motional EMF – Sliding Bar
$$\epsilon = BLv$$

| Symbol       | Meaning                                             | Units     |
| ------------ | --------------------------------------------------- | --------- |
| $$\epsilon$$ | Induced (motional) EMF                              | Volts (V) |
| B            | Uniform magnetic field strength                     | Tesla (T) |
| L            | Length of conductor (perpendicular to both B and v) | m         |
| v            | Speed of conductor                                  | m/s       |
#### Derivation from Faraday’s Law:
- Area swept per unit time = L • v, so: $$\frac{d \phi_{B}}{dt}=B\frac{dA}{dt}=BLv=BLv$$
#### Derivation from Lorentz Force (microscopic)
A free charge q inside the moving bar feels force: 
$$\vec{F}=q \vec{v} \times \vec{B}$$
This force separates charges along the bar, creating a potential difference = EMF
Work per unit charge = vBL → gives the same results.

#### Induced Current
*Only valid while the bar moves; current depends on instantaneous velocity v*
$$I=\frac{\epsilon}{R}=\frac{LBv}{R}$$
#### Magnetic Braking Force on a Bar
*The induced current **I** flows through the bar (length L) inside field B, producing a retarding force:* $$F_{brake}=BIL=\frac{L^2vB^2}{R}$$
- Direction: always opposes the motion (Lenz’s Law)
- Proportional to velocity → acts like velocity → ~~dependent drag~~
#### Force on a Current Carrying Conductor (General Formula)
$$\vec{F_{B}}=\int I(d \vec{l}\times \vec{B})$$
For a straight conductor of length L in a uniform field: $$F_{B}=BIL\sin \phi$$
where phi is the angle between the current direction and B. 
#### Power - Energy Conservation Check
Power delivered by the external force pushing a bar: $$P_{in}=F_{ext}*v$$
*Power dissipated as heat in a resistor*: $$P_{dissipated}=I^2R=\frac{L^2B^2v^2}{R}$$ Note: at a constant velocity energy is conserved.
#### Magnetic Force
$$F_{B}=\frac{NB^2a^2}{R}v=k$$
$$F=-kv$$
- *This is a prediction when substituting for k*
- This prediction acts as a confirmation for:
	- A changing magnetic flux through a conducting coil induces an emf that results in an induced current
	- The magnetic field exerts a force on wires as this induced current flows
- By applying Newton’s second law to the forces acting on a system, you can create a prediction of a velocity v. time graph. 
#### Eddy Currents
When a conductor (not a wire loop) moves through or is exposed to a changing magnetic (B) field, currents form in swirling closed loops inside the material. This is called an **Eddy Current**
- Obey Len’s Law: always oppose the change that caused them
- Dissipate energy as Joule Heating in the conductor’s resistance
- Create a force opposite to velocity
*Slicing the conductor into thin sheets with insulation in between each sheet increases the effective resistance of eddy current paths.*
