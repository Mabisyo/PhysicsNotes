# Concepts
_________________
*A changing magnetic flux through a closed loop induces an EMF and the resulting current always acts to oppose the change that caused it. Electromagnetic induction links changing magnetic fields to electric forces. The following two laws govern it completely:*
- **Faraday’s Law:** Gives the magnitude and sign of the induced emf (voltage)
- **Lenz’s Law:** gives the direction of the induced current
*Induction depends on the rate of change of flux, not on flux itself. A huge static B field induces zero emf. A tiny but rapidly changing field can induce a large amount of emf*
# Faraday’s Law
**A changing magnetic flux induces an emf (voltage) in the system**
	$$emf=-\frac{d\phi_{B}}{dt}$$

|Symbol|Meaning|Units|
|---|---|---|
|ε|Induced electromotive force (EMF)|Volts (V)|
|Φ_B|Magnetic flux through the loop|Weber (Wb)|
|dΦ_B/dt|Rate of change of flux|Wb/s = V|
|−|Lenz's Law (opposes the change)|—|

### Faraday's Law — N-Turn Coil

$$\varepsilon = -N\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt}$$

- Each turn of the coil "sees" the same flux change
- N turns multiplies the induced EMF by N
- A 100-turn coil produces 100× the EMF of a single loop under identical flux change

### Expanded Form (Calculus — AP Exam)

Since $\Phi_B = \int \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{A}$, Faraday's Law becomes:

$$\varepsilon = -\frac{d}{dt}\int_A \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{A}$$

For a flat loop with uniform B:

$$\varepsilon = -\frac{d}{dt}(BA\cos\theta)$$

This expands by the **product rule** — three ways flux can change:

$$\varepsilon = -\left(\frac{dB}{dt}A\cos\theta + B\frac{dA}{dt}\cos\theta + BA\left(-\sin\theta\right)\frac{d\theta}{dt}\right)$$
### Maxwell's Third Equation (Integral Form)

$$\oint \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{l} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt}$$

- A changing magnetic flux creates a **non-conservative electric field** around the loop
- This is the deeper meaning of Faraday's Law: it connects E fields and changing B fields
- This is one of **Maxwell's Four Equations** — foundational to all of electromagnetism
## Three Ways to Change Flux → Three Ways to Induce EMF

|Mechanism|What Changes|Example|
|---|---|---|
|**Change B**|dB/dt ≠ 0|Current in nearby wire increases|
|**Change Area**|dA/dt ≠ 0|Loop expands or conducting bar moves on rails|
|**Change Angle**|dθ/dt ≠ 0|Loop rotates in a magnetic field (generator)|

## 🧭 Lenz's Law

> **Lenz's Law:** The induced current flows in the direction that creates a magnetic field **opposing the change in flux** that caused it.

In three words: **"Oppose the change."**

The negative sign in Faraday's Law **is** Lenz's Law in mathematical form. Without it, energy conservation would be violated.

### Physical Basis

- If flux **increases** → induced B field opposes the increase (points opposite to external B)
- If flux **decreases** → induced B field opposes the decrease (points same direction as external B)
- The induced current always creates a force that **resists the motion** causing induction

### Energy Conservation Connection

When a magnet approaches a loop, the induced current repels it — you must do **work** to push the magnet in. That mechanical work becomes electrical energy, which dissipates as heat in the loop's resistance. Energy is conserved.

## 🔄 Step-by-Step Procedure: Finding Induced Current Direction

1. **Determine the direction of the original B field** through the loop
2. **Determine whether flux is increasing or decreasing**
3. **Apply Lenz's Law:** the induced B field must oppose the change
    - Flux increasing → induced B field points **opposite** to original B
    - Flux decreasing → induced B field points **same direction** as original B
4. **Apply the Right-Hand Rule** to find the current direction:
    - Point your thumb in the direction of the induced B field
    - Your fingers curl in the direction of the induced current

> [!example] Show your work on FRQs! Write out explicitly: _"Flux is increasing into the page → induced field must point out of the page → by right-hand rule, current flows counterclockwise."_ Graders award partial credit for correct reasoning even if the final diagram is wrong.

## 🔄 Rotating Loop — Generator Case

When a rectangular coil of area A and N turns rotates at angular velocity ω in a uniform field B:

$$\Phi_B(t) = NBA\cos(\omega t)$$

Differentiating:

$$\varepsilon(t) = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} = NBA\omega\sin(\omega t)$$

$$\varepsilon_0 = NBA\omega \quad \text{(peak EMF)}$$

> ⚠️ **Common Trap:** Maximum EMF occurs when the coil plane is **parallel** to the field (θ = 90° from normal → flux = 0, but _dΦ/dt_ is maximum). Maximum flux ≠ maximum EMF.

|Coil orientation|Flux|Rate of flux change|EMF|
|---|---|---|---|
|Coil ⊥ to B (face-on)|Maximum|Zero|**Zero**|
|Coil ∥ to B (edge-on)|Zero|Maximum|**Maximum**|


## 📊 Lenz's Law Scenario Quick Reference

|Scenario|Flux change|Induced B direction|Current (viewed from front)|
|---|---|---|---|
|Magnet (N pole) approaching|↑ increasing into page|Out of page|Counterclockwise|
|Magnet (N pole) receding|↓ decreasing into page|Into page|Clockwise|
|External B increasing (into page)|↑ increasing|Out of page|Counterclockwise|
|External B decreasing (into page)|↓ decreasing|Into page|Clockwise|
|Loop entering B field region|↑ increasing|Opposes B|Opposes motion|
|Loop exiting B field region|↓ decreasing|Supports B|Opposes motion|

## 🔗 Connection to Maxwell's Equations

Faraday's Law is **Maxwell's Third Equation**:

$$\oint \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{l} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt}$$

This states that a **time-varying magnetic field creates a circulating (non-conservative) electric field** — even in empty space with no conductor present. This was one of Maxwell's key insights that unified electricity and magnetism.

Maxwell's Four Equations recap:

1. $\oint \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A} = \frac{Q_{enc}}{\varepsilon_0}$ — Gauss's Law (E)
2. $\oint \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{A} = 0$ — Gauss's Law (B)
3. $\oint \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{l} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt}$ — **Faraday's Law** ← this lesson
4. $\oint \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{l} = \mu_0 I_{enc} + \mu_0\varepsilon_0\frac{d\Phi_E}{dt}$ — Ampere-Maxwell Law

## 🎯 AP Exam Tips

> [!tip] Exam Strategy
> 
> - **Always state Faraday's Law** before applying it in FRQ responses — even one line: "By Faraday's Law, ε = −dΦ_B/dt"
> - For direction questions, write out your Lenz's Law reasoning **step by step** — partial credit is awarded for the logic chain even if your final direction is wrong
> - For N-turn coils, **don't forget the N** — it's easy to miss under exam pressure
> - When differentiating flux, use the **product rule** if B, A, and θ are all time-varying
> - For rotating coil problems: set up $\Phi_B = NBA\cos(\omega t)$, then differentiate — don't memorize the result, derive it

> [!warning] Common Mistakes
> 
> - **Confusing maximum flux with maximum EMF** — they occur at opposite moments in a rotating coil
> - **Forgetting the negative sign** — it's not just notation; it determines current direction
> - Using $\Delta\Phi / \Delta t$ instead of $d\Phi/dt$ when B changes non-linearly — must use calculus
> - Applying Lenz's Law to the wrong quantity — always ask "what is the _change_ in flux?" not just "what is the flux?"
> - Confusing the **induced B field direction** with the **induced current direction** — use the right-hand rule to connect them

## 📝 Key Vocabulary

|Term|Definition|
|---|---|
|**Induced EMF (ε)**|EMF generated by a changing magnetic flux; measured in Volts|
|**Faraday's Law**|ε = −dΦ_B/dt; rate of flux change drives induced EMF|
|**Lenz's Law**|The induced current opposes the change in flux that caused it|
|**Back-EMF**|EMF induced in a coil that opposes the change in current (motors, inductors)|
|**Flux Linkage (NΦ)**|Total flux threading an N-turn coil; NΦ_B|
|**Non-conservative E field**|Electric field produced by changing B — cannot be described by a potential|
|**Maxwell's Third Equation**|Integral form of Faraday's Law; ∮E·dl = −dΦ_B/dt|
