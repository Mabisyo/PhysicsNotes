# The Concept of Electric Potential
- In 9.1, we used the concept of Equal Potential **Energy**, which is directly proportionate to the charge of a specific charge and/or system. 
- However say you wanted to describe an area of space’s ability to do work on an object, regardless of what charge is in that space. This describes the concept of Electric Potential, the ability of a location in space to transfer energy to a charge placed there, and what differentiates it from Electric Potential Energy.
	- Think of this concept as water pressure inside of a pipe. If the water is flowing or not, the pressure still exists inside of the pipe. When the water pressure does flow, the pressure drives it and does work on it. Higher pressure means there is more ability to push the water and do work.
	- Electric Potential would represent the pressure, and the flow would represent the charge. When the charge does arrive, the Electric potential determines how much energy gets transferred to that charge.
- The electric potential is measured in Volts. (This is not Voltage, that is Potential Difference).

# Electric Potential Equation
$$V=\frac{U_E}{q}$$
- Where U_E is the electric potential energy, V is for voltage, and q is the test particle, meaning if you have your Electric Potential, you can find your particle’s electric potential energy, and vice versa. This comes from dividing the test particle away from our original equation.
$$U_E=\frac{1}{4\pi \epsilon_0}\frac{q_{test}q_{source}}{r}$$
$$V=\frac{U_E}{q_{test}}=\frac{1}{4\pi \epsilon_0}\frac{q_{source}}{r}$$

# Superposition of Electric Potential
- Similar to other quantities in this course, when trying to find the total Electric Potential, you add up each individual voltages, as shown below.
$$V_{total}=\frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_0} \sum\frac{q_i}{r_i}$$

- Keep in mind, radius r is with respect to our origin. For example, if we have charge 1 at x=0, and charge 2 at x=4, and we want to find the Electric Potential total at point P at x=2, then both our radii would be 2, since both are 2 units away from point P.



# Integral Form of Electric Potential
$$V=\frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_0} \int\frac{dq}{r}$$
- Notice how Electric Potential does not have an r hat. This is because Electric Potential is a scalar quantity, it has no direction, and therefore no need for and r hat.
- This derivation will follow the same substitution principles as Gauss’s law, and derivations in 8.4 involving charge density.
- IMPORTANT: College Board only expects you to use this integral for the same distributions as in 8.4, being an infinitely long wire, a thin ring along its axis, a semicircular arc at its center, and a finite wire at specific points.



# The Relationship between D and E
- In this section, there are numerous mathematical “buzzwords” that extend pass the grasp of Calculus AB and BC, however the concepts are nothing different than what we have worked with before. Though the notation and terminology might be scary, the concepts are the exact same as we have done before.
$$E_s=-\frac{dV}{ds}$$
- The electric field is the negative spatial derivative of the electric potential. What this means is the derivative of Electric Potential with respect to distance is the electric field. A spatial derivative is the rate of change of a quantity with respect to distance, rather than time. There is nothing new here, we simply treat our distance as our variables, and treat time as a constant, similar to defining other quantities as constants in other problems.

- We can also denote this same formula in our 3 dimensions using partial derivatives, which basically means we can measure the rate of change of a multivariable function with respect to one specific variable (in this case, 1 dimension), while holding all other variables (other dimensions, such as x and y) constant.
$$E_x=\frac{\partial V}{\partial x}$$$$E_y=\frac{\partial V}{\partial y}$$$$E_z=\frac{\partial V}{\partial z}$$

- Recall that positive charges naturally move from high potential to low potential, or from High V to Low V (just like how an object released from up high starts with a high gravitational potential energy and decreases in gravitational potential energy as it falls), The relationship between Electric Field and Electric Potential has a negative sign.


- To go in the opposite direction, from E to V, requires integration.
$$\Delta V=V_B-V_A=-\int^B_A{\vec E}*d\vec s$$
- Note that we only take into account the final positions of the particles A and B. This is because the electric force is conservative, it is independent of the path taken to get there, the only thing that matters is the final positions.
	- The most famous example of this is gravity. When you lift a book 1m, gravity does the same amount of negative work if you lifted the book directly upward, diagonally, or went in circles. 
	- The electric force behaves identically. Moving a charge from point A to point B, the work done by the electric force depends only on where A and B are, not on how you got from one to the other.



# Formal Voltage Definition
- The potential difference, or delta V is more commonly referred to as Voltage. This is the what physically drives charges to move. 
$$\Delta V=V_B-V_A=\frac{\Delta U_E}{q}$$
- The term Voltage will always refer to a difference, never an actual value. 
- By convention in most problems, Delta V=0 at infinity (for point charges).
- A charge only moves spontaneously if there is a potential difference. A positive charge moves from high potential to low potential (in the direction of the Electric field). A negative charge moves from low potential to high potential (opposite to the Electric field).

- Since we know the change in Electric potential energy is negative work, we can find our new definition below as…
$$-W_{Electric}=W_{Extrernal}=q\Delta V$$
- Remember that any external for Work is equal and opposite from the Work done by the electric field.
## Electron Volt
  $$1 eV=qV=(1.6×10^{−19} C)(1 V)=1.6×10^{−19} J$$
- This is a common unit used in particle physics problems on the AP Exam.


# Equipotential Lines
![[equipotentiallinesfor9.2.png]]
- A equipotential line is a line connecting all points in space that have all the same electric potential, V.
- For uniform electric fields (displayed above), equipotential lines are concentric circles around a source. Meaning, all points on a single circle have the same electric potential.
- This means that moving from one point on a circle to another requires no work, which satisfies our equation from earlier. 
$$W=q\Delta V$$
- The E Field is ALWAYS PERPENDICULAR to the Equipotential Lines. NO EXCEPTION. Equipotential Lines will never cross each other.

- The most common analogy to understand equipotential lines is pictures of topographical maps.
![[topographicmapanalogyfor9.2.png]]
- Every single line represents an area of land with the same elevation. To move along a line, your elevation would not change (requiring no work), but to move from line to line, that requires a change in elevation (or a change in work).

- Physicists conventionally tend to draw each concentric circle separated by the same delta V. This means that to get from one circle to another, it requires the same work, however this is not always the case on the AP exam. You can normally assume the delta V is the same, unless the question gives you information proving they are not.
- We can pull a lot of information out of Equipotential Line Diagrams
	- Closely placed Equipotential lines = Strong Field
	- Spaced out Equipotential Lines = Weak Field
	- Delta V = Outside Line V - Inside Line V
	- Field Strength decreases as you move away = Lines space out

- Field lines always point from high to low potential. Positive charges naturally accelerate from high V to low V, in the direction of the field. Negative charges do the opposite.



## Equipotential Lines for a Dipole
- An Electric dipole is a system of 2 charges with equal magnitude but opposite charges (more in …).
![[dipoleelectricfieldexample.png]]
- In this picture above, the red lines represent the electric field lines. Though maybe hard to see, notice the arrows pointing towards the negative charge, and away from the positive ones.
- The green lines represent the equipotential lines.
	- Near both charges, equipotential lines are crowded, the electric field is strong.
	- Far from both charges, equipotential lines are spread out, the electric field is weak.
	- Exactly halfway between the charges, there is a vertical line. This is known as the Zero-Potential line, which has zero electric potential.




# Worked Conceptual/Mathematical Example
- If you wanted to find the electric potential for a thin ring of radius R carries total charge -Q uniformly distributed at a distance x from the center, at point P.![[pointalongtheaxisofaringelectricfield.png]]
- This is the same example as 8.4, but instead used for Electric Potential, 

1. Start by finding a substitution for r.
$$r=\sqrt{x^2+R^2}$$
2. Since we have a uniformly distrusted Q, we have no need for any substitutions. 
$$V=\frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_0} \int\frac{dq}{r}$$
$$V=\frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_0}\frac{1}{\sqrt{x^2+R^2}} \int_0^{-Q} dq$$

$$V=\frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_0}\frac{-Q}{\sqrt{x^2+R^2}}$$

- Since we have our V, we can find our E-Field using derivatives.
$$E_x=\frac{\partial V}{\partial x}$$
$$E=\frac{\partial }{\partial x}\left(\frac{1}{4  \pi \epsilon_0}\frac{-Q}{\sqrt{x^2+R^2}}\right) $$
$$E=\frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_0}\frac{-xQ}{({x^2+R^2})^{\frac{3}{2}}}\hat r$$
- Some may find this approach to the question from 8.4 easier, due to simpler calculus concepts, but it is entirely up to preference.
