# What is a Capacitor?
- In circuits and other electronics, capacitors are common piece of hardware responsible for making things work. They work by storing a known amount of charge and allowing it to be released whenever necessary.
- It’s built by holding 2 conducting plates at a distance apart from each other, separated by an insulating material or just free space (vacuum). Each plate has a different electric potential, meaning the capacitor itself stores charge within a potential difference.
- When the capacitor is connected to a source of potential difference (like a battery), charge builds up on the plates. The two charges of each of the plates are equal and opposite. This is because charge flows from one plate to the other through an external circuit (Unit 11), so for every positive charge that accumulates on one plate, one negative charge accumulates on the other.
---


# Capacitance
- Capacitance is a measure of how much charge a capacitor can store per volt of potential difference. It is purely a geometric property of the capacitor it depends only on the physical construction, not on the charge or voltage. Think of it as a ratio, if you have a capacitor of capacitance C, then you can only have a charge Q for some $\Delta$V. The equation is given by…$${C = \frac{Q}{\Delta V}}$$

- Where… 
	- C is the capacitance in (Farads, F)
	- Q  magnitude of charge on either plate (Coulombs)
	- ΔV potential difference between the plates (Volts)
- The unit of capacitance is the Farad: C/V
- One Farad is an enormous capacitance, typical capacitors in electronics are measured in microfarads (μF = 10⁻⁶ F) or picofarads (pF = 10⁻¹² F).

- What does this equation tell us?
	- A larger capacitance value means that the capacitor can store a larger charge with a smaller potential difference between the plates. As mentioned before, the geometry and distance between the 2 conductors play a large role in the Capacitance value. See more below.

- Capacitance is measured using a multimeter.

# The Parallel Plate Capacitor
- The most common form of capacitors tested on the AP Exam is the 2 parallel plate capacitor, which consists of 2 parallel plates separated by insulating material. 
	- FYI: Different capacitors exist, such as 2 plates rolled into each other, to preserve space. Their geometry is complex, and not tested on the AP Exam.
- In the following derivation we can find Capacitance in terms of Area “A” of the plates separated by distance “d,” 

1. First, we can recall the use of Gauss’s Law to find the electric field of a sheet, when defining a Gaussian Surface as a pillbox. Since we are working with just two plates, the equation disregards the 1/2. This is the equation for the electric field in a capacitor. The Equation is as follows…
$$E = \frac{\sigma}{\varepsilon_0} = \frac{Q}{\varepsilon_0 A}$$
2. We can recall the definition of potential difference. Since we know that E-Field is constant, we can simply multiply our E by distance d. 
$$|\Delta V|=\left|-{\int^B_A{\vec E}*d\vec s}\right|=E*d$$​
	- Why the absolute value? When working with potential difference, the signifies that the potential difference decreases in the direction of E, meaning the electric field moves from the positive to the negative plate. The negative sign determines direction, but we already know the direction of the electric field, since we will know which plate is +q and which is -q, so we can ignore the direction.

3. Since we solved for E-Field already, we can substitute it in for Potential difference, leaving us with…
$$\Delta V= \frac{Qd}{\epsilon_0A}$$


4. We can now plug this into the original capacitance equation, leaving us with…$$C=\frac{Q}{\frac{Qd}{\epsilon_0A}}=\frac{\epsilon_0A}{d}$$

- What does this equation tell us?
	- Larger Area = Larger Capacitance. This is because a larger area allows more room for charges to spread out, meaning for the same potential difference, more charge can accumulate.
	- Smaller Separation = Larger Capacitance. Bringing the plates closer together means the electric force is stronger between the two plates. Since the charges hold onto each other more tightly, more charge can accumulate.

# Electric Potential Energy in a Capacitor
- Now we can loop in Electric potential energy, allowing us to relate all the variables into the following equation. 
- Conceptually, when you charge a capacitor, you must move charge from one plate to the other against the growing potential difference. Initially, there is little to no $\Delta$V, so the charge can move from one plate to another easier, but as $\Delta$V accumulates, the charge gets harder to transfer, requiring more work. The derivation begins with…
$$dW=U_E=qV=\Delta V \cdot dq$$
$$dW  = \frac{q}{C}dq$$

- We can integrate both sides to find,

$$W=U_C = \int_0^Q \frac{q}{C}dq = \frac{1}{C}\int_0^Q q\,dq = \frac{1}{C}\cdot\frac{Q^2}{2}$$​

- Therefore we are left with all of these equations, that are all equal to each other.

$${U_C = \frac{Q^2}{2C} = \frac{1}{2}C(\Delta V)^2 = \frac{1}{2}Q\Delta V}$$​




## 10.3.8 — Energy Stored in the Electric Field
- When talking about the energy in an electric field, it isn’t stored in the charges themselves, but rather by the electric field itself. In a capacitor, the electric field resides between the plates.
- We can derive a formula for the density of energy within an electric field by combining the 3 following equations into 1.
$$U_c=\frac{1}{2}C(\Delta V)^2$$$$C=\frac{\epsilon_0A}{d}$$$$\Delta V=Ed$$
$$u_e=\frac{1}{2}\epsilon_0 E^2=\frac{V_{volume}}{U_c}$$
- This formula, though derived with capacitance concepts, is applicable for all electric fields.



# Capacitors in Circuits
- Though circuits haven’t been fully introduced yet, capacitors can be in combination through either series or parallel.


- In parallel circuits, both capacitors have the same voltage, and…
$$C_{total}=C_1+C_2+...$$


- However in series, capacitors work very differently….
$$\Delta V_{total}=\Delta V_1+\Delta V_2 +....$$
$$\frac{1}{C_{total}}=\frac{1}{C_{1}}+\frac{1}{C_{2}}+...$$


- What happens if a capacitor is connected to a battery, vs disconnected?

| Situation               | Held Constant | Adjusts    |
| ----------------------- | ------------- | ---------- |
| Connected to battery    | ΔV            | Q and U_C  |
| Isolated (disconnected) | Q             | ΔV and U_C |
