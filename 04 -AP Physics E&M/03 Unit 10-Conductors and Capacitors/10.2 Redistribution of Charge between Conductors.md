# Three Mechanisms of Charge Redistribution
- When 2 conductors are brought near each other or physically connected, and there is a potential difference between them, then charges redistribute themselves until both conductors reach the same potential difference. Once both conductors have the same electric potential, Delta V = 0, and the charges stop moving, leaving them both in electrostatic equilibrium. 

- There are three distinct ways conductors can interact and redistribute charge. Each produces a different outcome.


## Direct Contact (Conduction)

- When two conductors are brought into physical contact, they effectively become one conductor. Charge redistributes across both until the entire combined system reaches a single uniform potential.
- When the two conductors are separated, 2 things remain true. The total charge remains constant (conservation of charge), and both conductors have the same electric potential. Say you have conductor 1 and conductor 2, then…
$$Q_1 + Q_2 = Q_1' + Q_2' = Q_{total}$$$$V_1' = V_2'$$​
- Say we have 2 conducting spheres of radius 1 and 2, then the potential of each sphere after contact is…

$$V = \frac{kQ_1'}{R_1} = \frac{kQ_2'}{R_2}​​$$
- We can cancel our Ks and we are left with….
$$\frac{Q_1'}{R_1}=\frac{Q_2'}{R_2}$$
$$Q_2'=Q_1'\frac{R_2}{R_1}$$
- We can derive a new equation for the charges of Q1’ and Q2’ by starting with our conservation of charge.
$$Q_1'+Q_2'=Q_{total}$$
$$Q_1'+Q_1'\frac{R_2}{R_1}=Q_{total}$$
$$Q_1'(1+\frac{R_2}{R_1})=Q_{total}$$
$$Q_1'(\frac{R_1}{R_1}+\frac{R_2}{R_1})=Q_{total}$$
$$Q_1'(\frac{R_1+R_2}{R_1})=Q_{total}$$
$$Q_1'=Q_{total}(\frac{R_1}{R_1+R_2})$$
- You can do the same but isolate Q2’ and get the following
$$Q_2'=Q_{total}(\frac{R_2}{R_1+R_2})$$
- Say conductor 2 is the larger conductor, that means that R2 > R1. Therefore, the larger sphere gets more charge. Charge distributes proportionally to radius, the larger the sphere, so it accommodates more charge.
- This is a general equation for all spherical conductors that come into contact.

## Induction Without Contact
- As discussed in 8.2, we can use induction and grounding to charge a conductor, using the following steps.
	1. Bring a charged object of +Q towards the right side (Frame of reference) of a neutral conductor.
	2. The conductor becomes polarized, electrons move to the right side of the conductor.
	3. There is an electric field created by the absence of electrons on the left side of the conductor perfectly cancels the external field created by the charged object. E-Field remains zero inside the conductor.
	4. Grounding the conductor will cause electrons to flow into the conductor, and when the conductor is ungrounded, there will be an overall net negative charge on the surface of the conductor when the charged object moves away.
	5. If the same is done but the object has a -Q, then electrons will flow outside the conductor when grounded, leaving the conductor with a net positive on the surface of the conductor.

## Grounding
- When a charged conductor is grounded:
	- If the conductor was at V > 0: electrons flow from Earth onto the conductor, reducing its positive charge until V = 0
	- If the conductor was at V < 0: electrons flow from the conductor to Earth, reducing its negative charge until V = 0

- In either case, the conductor ends up at V = 0 (due to the fact the Earth has V = 0) with whatever charge is necessary to achieve that potential.

# The Potential of Connected Conductors
- When two conductors are connected by a thin wire, they form one combined conductor and must reach the same potential. This principle is what defines voltage in circuits, every wire in a circuit is a conductor, all points connected by wire are at the same potential. Potential differences only exist across components that change charge flow, such as resistors, capacitors, inductors. The wire itself causes zero potential difference.
