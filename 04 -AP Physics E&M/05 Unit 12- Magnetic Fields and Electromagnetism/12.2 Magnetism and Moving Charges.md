# Concepts
________
### Moving Charges Produce Magnetic Fields
- A moving electric charge creates a magnetic field in the surrounding space. This is the fundamental origin of all magnetism → Even permanent magnets owe their magnetism to electron spin. Stationary charges do not produce magnetic fields, only electric fields. 
#### For a Single Moving Charge
- For a single point charge q moving with velocity v, the field direction is found by the simplified cross product direction rule: $$\vec{B} \propto \vec{v} \times \hat{r}$$
- Here, v is the velocity of the charge and **r** is the unit vector pointing from the charge to the field point.
#### Right Hand Rule #1: Finding the Direction of B from a Moving Positive Charge
1. Point your pointer finger in the direction of velocity of charge or direction of current
2. Point your middle finger in the direction source → field point
3. Thumb points in direction of vector B

#### Right Hand Rule #2: For Circular Field Pattern
1. Thumb: Point in direction of velocity (current)
2. Curled fingers: show the direction the field circles around the moving charge. The field forms closed loops around the charge’s path
#### Shape of the Magnetic Field
*The Magnetic Field of a Moving Charge Forms Concentric Circles (Loops) Centered on the Velocity Vector:*
- The field is strongest closest to the charge and falls off as 1/r^2
- The field lines are always closed loops → magnetic monopoles do not exist 
- At a point directly in front of or behind the moving charge (along the current), the field is zero because the cross product of vector r and vector v is zero when vectors are parallel.
- Maximum field strength is at points perpendicular to the velocity.
## Force On a Charged Particle Moving Through a Magnetic Field
#### The Magnetic Force Law (Lorentz Force → Magnetic Part)
$$\vec{F}_{B}=q \vec{V} \times \vec{B}$$
With magnitude as follows: $$F_{B}=qvB\sin \theta$$
- F_B → Magnetic force vector on the charged particle
- q → Charge of the particle (Sign matters)
- Vector V → velocity vector of the particle
- Vector B → Magnetic field vector at particle’s location
- Theta → Angle between vector v and vector B
#### When is the Force Zero?
• **Charge at rest:** v = 0, so F_B = 0. A static charge in a magnetic field feels nothing.

• **Velocity parallel to B⃗:** θ = 0° or 180°, so sinθ = 0, F_B = 0. Motion along field lines produces no force.

• **Maximum force when v⃗ ⊥ B⃗:** θ = 90°, sinθ = 1, F_B = qvB.

#### Right Hand Rule #3: Direction of Magnetic Force
1. Pointer Finger → direction of velocity
2. Middle Finger → direction of magnetic field
3. Thumb → direction of the force of the magnetic field for a positive charge.
	1.  For an electron: apply same steps and reverse direction
#### Properties of the Magnetic Force
- Always Perpendicular to the particle’s speed → means it cannot change the particle’s speed, only its direction.
- Does no work
- Acts as centripetal force
- Proportional to charge magnitude speed, field strength, sin(theta)
#### Circular Motion in a Uniform Magnetic Field 
$$\begin{array}
 \\  1. \space qvB = \frac{mv^2}{r} 
 \\  2. \space r = \frac{mv}{qB}
 \\  3. \space f = \frac{qB}{2\pi m}
 \\  4. \space T = \frac{2\pi m}{qB}
\end{array}$$
2. Radius of circular path
3. Cyclotron frequency
4. Period
###### Magnetic Force Never Does Work
- *On the AP exam, you may be asked whether B does work on a particle. The answer is always no. Even though B causes circular motion, it only redirects the velocity → it never speeds up or slows down the particle. All kinetic energy changes require an electric field or another force.*
#### Velocity at an Angle to Vector B (Helical Motion)
- When the velocity has both a component parallel and perpendicular to B
	- v|| (parallel component): Not affected by B. Particle continues with constant velocity in this direction.
	- v perpendicular → Causes circular motion around B direction
	- Combined: The particle traces a helical path centered on the field direction. This explains how charged particles spiral along Earth’s magnetic field lines
### Hall Effect
- Determines charge carrier sign: The polarity of the Hall voltage tells you whether current carriers are positive or negative → historically this confirmed electrons are the carriers in metals. 
- Measures B field: Hall sensors are used in everything from smartphone compasses to motor controllers.
- Measures drift velocity: V_H directly relates to how fast charge carriers move through the material. 
**Note:** **⚠ Electron Direction vs. Conventional Current**
	In Hall Effect problems, always be careful: conventional current I and electron drift velocity v_d point in OPPOSITE directions. When applying the force law to electrons (q = −e), either: (1) use the actual electron velocity and keep q = −e, or (2) use RHR for positive current direction and then reverse. Both methods give the same answer — but mixing them up will give you the wrong polarity of the Hall voltage.
