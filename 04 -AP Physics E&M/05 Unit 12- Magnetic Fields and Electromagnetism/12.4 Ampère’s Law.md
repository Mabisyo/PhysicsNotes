# Concepts

### Ampere’s Law
- Ampere’s Law is the magnetic analogue of Gauss’s Law for electric fields. While Gauss’s Law relates surface integral of E to enclose charge, Ampere’s Law relates a line integral of B around a closed loop to the current passing through the surface bounded by that loop. It is one of Maxwell’s four equations and is the go-to tool whenever a current distribution has sufficient symmetry to make the line integral tractable. 
$$\oint \vec{B} \cdot d \vec{I}=\mu_{0} I_{enc}$$
- *The closed line integral of B around any closed path equals mu sub zero times the net current passing through the surface bounded by that path.*

| Variables  | Meaning                                                                      | Units   |
| ---------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------- |
| ∮ B⃗ · dI⃗ | Closed line integral of B along the Amperian Loop                            | T*m     |
| μ₀         | Permeability of free space                                                   | T*(m/A) |
| I_enc      | Net current threading the surface bounded by the loop (sign from RHR)        | A       |
| dI⃗        | Infinitesimal length element along the closed path (vector, tangent to path) | m       |
#### Choosing the Sign of I_enc
- Use the right-hand rule: curl the fingers of your right-hand in the direction you traverse the Amperian loop. Your thumb points in the direction that defines positive current. Current in that thumb direction = +I_enc; current opposite = −I_enc. The net I_enc is the algebraic sum of all currents through the surface. The choice of direction of traversal is arbitrary — but once chosen, it fixes the sign convention for all currents in that problem.
### Requirements for Using Ampere’s Law
- Ampere’s Law is ALWAYS true, but it is only useful when the current distribution has symmetries:

| Symmetry Type          | Example Geometry                               | Best Amperian Loop Shape     | Result                                       |
| ---------------------- | ---------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------- | -------------------------------------------- |
| Cylindrical (axial)    | Long straight wire, cylinder, or coaxial cable | Circle centered on wire axis | B = μ₀I_enc / (2πr)                          |
| Translational (linear) | Infinite solenoid, very long solenoid          | Rectangle                    | B = μ₀nI  inside; B ≈ 0 outside              |
| Planar                 | Infinite current sheet or conducting slab      | Rantangle                    | B = μ₀K/2  (K = surface current density A/m) |
#### Magnetic Field of a Long Straight Wire
**Thin Wire (Ideal/Infinitely Thin Conductor)**
$$B = \frac{\mu_{0}I}{2\pi r}$$
*Steps:*
1. Draw a circular Amperian loop of radius r around the wire. The current passes straight through the loop.
2. By symmetry, B is constant and tangential
3. Apply and solve Ampere’s Law
#### Thick Cylindrical Conductor (Solid, Uniform)
*A solid cylindrical conductor of radius R carries total current I with uniform current density:* $$J= \frac{I}{\pi r^2}$$
- If **Outside the Conductor, the entire current is enclosed and is identical to the thin wire case:**
- If **Indside the Conductor (r <R)** The Amperian Loop (circle of radius r), only encloses the fraction of current proportional to the area ratio: $$I_{enc} = J \times \pi r^2=\frac{I}{\pi R^2}\times \pi r^2 = I(\frac{r^2}{R^2})$$ $$B(2\pi r) = \mu_{0}I_{enc}=\frac{\mu_{0} I r^2}{R^2}$$ $$B = (\frac{\mu_{0} I_{enc}}{2\pi R^2}\times r) [r<R]$$
- At surface: $$B = \frac{\mu_{0}I}{2\pi r}$$
#### Hollow Cylindrical Conductor (Pipe/Shell)
*A hollow conducting shell with inner radius R1 and outer radius R2 carries total current I uniformly distributed through the material. Current Density:* $$J = \frac{I}{\pi (R_{2}^2-R_{1}^2)}$$

| Region                    | Enclosed Current | Result                                 |
| ------------------------- | ---------------- | -------------------------------------- |
| r< R1                     | 0                | B=0 (no enclosed current)              |
| R1<r<R2 (Inside Material) | J × π(r² − R₁²)  | B = μ₀ I (r² − R₁²) / [2πr(R₂² − R₁²)] |
| r>R2 (outside material)   | I (full current) | B = μ₀ I / (2πr)                       |
#### Coaxial Cable
*Inner solid conductor (radius a) carries current I outward. Outer shell (inner radius b, outer radius c) carries return current I inward. Uniform J in each conductor.*

| Region                          | Net Enclosed Current    | B                    |
| ------------------------------- | ----------------------- | -------------------- |
| r < a (inside inner)            | I(r²/a²)  (partial)     | B = μ₀ I r / (2π a²) |
| a < r < b (between conductors)  | +I  (full inner only)   | B = μ₀ I / (2πr)     |
| b < r < c  (inside outer shell) | +I − I[(r²−b²)/(c²−b²)] | Partial cancellation |
| r > c  (outside all)            | +I − I = 0              | B=0                  |
### The Ideal Solenoid
- A solenoid is a coil of wire wound in a tight helix. When current flows, each turn produces a magnetic field, and all of the turns’ fields add constructively along the ais inside the solenoid. For an ideal (Infinitely long) solenoid:
	- **Inside** the magnetic field is uniform, constant and parallel to the solenoid axis
	- **Outside** the magnetic field is about zero (field lines that come out the end spread infinitely and become negligibly small)
	- **At the end:** Magnetic field is approximately half of the interior value
### Defining n –– Turns Per Unit Length
$$n=\frac{N}{L}$$ Where N = total number of turns, L = length of solenoid, also written as: 
#### Ideal Solenoid
$$B = \mu_{0}nI = \mu_{0}(\frac{N}{L})I$$
#### What Changes B Inside a Solenoid?

| Change Made                               | Effect on B        | Reason                                      |
| ----------------------------------------- | ------------------ | ------------------------------------------- |
| Double the current I                      | B Doubles          | B ∝ I (direct proportion)                   |
| Double the number of tuns N (same length) | B Doubles          | n doubles, B = μ₀nI doubles                 |
| Double the length L (same N)              | B Halves           | n = N/L halves                              |
| Double both N and L (n unchanged)         | B unchanged        | n stays the same                            |
| Double the radius of solenoid             | B unchanged        | B = μ₀nI has no radius dependence           |
| Add iron core (μ_r >> 1)                  | B increases by μ_r | Replace μ₀ with μ = μ_r μ₀ (not on AP exam) |
### The Toroid (Donut Solenoid)
**A toroid is a solenoid bent into a closed loop – a donut shape. It has N total turns wound uniformly around a ring of inner radius r1 and outer radius r2. The field is confined entirely inside the toroid – outside including the hole in the center B = 0**

#### Toroid
Inside: $$\begin{array}
 \\ r_{1}<r<r_{2} \space inside: 
 \\ B = \frac{\mu_{0NI}}{2\pi r}
 \\ r<r_{1} or r>r_{2}, B=0 \space Outside
\end{array}$$
### Ampere’s Law Problem Solving Strategy

**Step 1: Identify the symmetry.**

Look at the current distribution. Is it cylindrical (wire, thick cylinder, coaxial cable)? Translational (solenoid)? Planar (current sheet)? If the symmetry is high enough, Ampère's Law will work. If not, you need Biot-Savart.

**Step 2: Choose the Amperian loop.**

Pick a closed path where B is either: (a) parallel to dI⃗ and constant in magnitude along part of the path, or (b) perpendicular to dI⃗ (contributing 0) along other parts. Common choices: circles for cylindrical symmetry, rectangles for solenoids/slabs.

**Step 3: Evaluate the line integral.**

Since B is constant along the useful portions and perpendicular (zero) elsewhere, the integral simplifies to B × (path length). For a circular loop: ∮ B⃗ · dI⃗ = B(2πr). For a rectangular loop in a solenoid: ∮ = Bℓ.

**Step 4: Calculate I_enc.**

Find the net current passing through the surface bounded by your loop. For uniform J: I_enc = J × A_enc. For non-uniform J: I_enc = ∫ J · dA. Determine sign by RHR.

**Step 5: Solve for B.**

Set your line integral equal to μ₀ I_enc and solve algebraically. Combine with direction from RHR.
