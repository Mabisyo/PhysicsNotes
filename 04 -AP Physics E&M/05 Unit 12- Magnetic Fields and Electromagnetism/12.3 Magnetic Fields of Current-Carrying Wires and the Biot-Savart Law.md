#### Why does a Magnetic Field Exert Force on a Wire?
- A current-carrying wire experiences a magnetic force because current consists of moving charges. A moving charge in a magnetic field experiences a force. When charges flow through a wire, those moving charges collectively experience a force, which is transmitted to the wire itself.
#### Magnetic Force Equation
$$dF_{B} = I(d\vec{l} \times B)$$
- The derivative of magnetic force over a tiny segment
- *I* is the magnitude of the current in the wire (in Amperes)
- Derivate of length is a small vector segment of the wire, pointing in the direction of conventional current flow
- Vector B is the magnetic field vector at the location of that wire segment. 

**To find the net force on the entire wire, integrate over all segments (Along the Wire):**
$$\vec{F_{b}}=\int I(d \vec{l} \times \vec{B})$$
#### Straight wire in a Uniform Field
- When the wire is straight and the magnetic field is uniform (constant direction and magnitude), the cross product simplifies. Using cross product, we can remove constants from the integral.
**The Integral simplifies to:** $$F_{B}=IlB\sin \theta$$
- This formula only applies when (1) the wire is straight, and (2) the magnetic field is uniform in both strength and direction. This formula is not on the equation sheet and must be derived from the integral form. 

### Finding Direction: Right Hand Rule

| Step   | Action                                                              |
| ------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Step 1 | Point your index finger in the direction of current (I)             |
| Step 2 | Point your middle finger in the direction of the magnetic field (B) |
| Step 3 | Your thumb now points in the direction of force (F_B)               |
### Currents Create Magnetic Fields
- Just as a charge at rest creates and electric field, a moving charge (current) creates a magnetic field. 
#### Shape of the Magnetic Field: Concentric Circles
*The magnetic field created by a long, straight current-carrying wire forms concentric circles centered on the wire.*
- Field lines are closed loops (circles) centered on the wire
- Magnetic field vectors are tangent to these circular field lines
- The field has no component directed toward, away from, or along the wire itself. 
- Field strength is greatest close to the wire and weakens with distance
- Field strength increases as current increases (directly proportional)
## Biot-Savart Law
- Fundamental equation that quantitatively describes the magnetic field produced by a tiny segment of current-carrying wire. It is the magnetic analog of Coulomb’s Law for electric fields. $$d \vec{B} = \frac{(\frac{\mu_{0}}{4\pi})\times I(d \vec{l} \times \hat{r})}{r^2}$$
	- Derivative of Magnetic Field → infinitesimal magnetic field contribution from one wire segment
	- Mu sub 0 → permeability of free space (constant)
	- *I* current in the wire
	- Derivative of l → Infinitesimal length vector pointing in the direction of current
	- r hat → unit vector from the wire segment to the point of interest
	- r → Distance from the wire segment to point of interest
- To find the total field, integrate over the entire wire: $$\vec{B} = \frac{\mu_{0}}{4\pi} \int \frac{I\sin \theta dl}{r^2} $$
##### Magnetic Field of a Long Straight Wire
- By applying the Biot-Savart Law to a wire of infinite length and integrating from negative infinity to positive infinity using trigonometric substitutions, the result simplifies to: $$|B_{P}|= \frac{\mu{0}I}{2\pi r \perp}$$
	- The field is directly proportional to current I → more current means stronger field
	- The field is inversely proportional to perpendicular distance → field falls off as 1/r
	- The 1/r dependence is different from the electric field of a point charge
	- Direction is found using the right hand rule: thumb along current, fingers curl in direction of B
##### Magnetic Field at Center of a Current Loop
- For a single circular loop of wire with radius r carrying current I, the Biot-Savart Law simplifies at the center
	- Current I is constant throughout the loop → remove from integral
	- Every point on the loop is the same distance r from the center → r is constant, remove from integral
	- sin of theta is 1 always $$|B_{P}=\frac{\mu_{0} I}{(2r)}|$$
##### Superposition
- When multiple wire segments contribute to the magnetic field at a point, the net field is the vector sum of the individual contributions. This is the principle of superposition → the same principle that applies to electric fields. 
	- For a loop: each tiny section dl creates its own dB field
	- These integrate to the total field B
	- At the center of a symmetric loop, by symmetry, all contributions point the same direction
